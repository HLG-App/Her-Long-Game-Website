Why this exists

The financial system was not
built with us in mind.

For generations, women were left out of financial conversations, investment decisions, wealth-building systems, and the language of money itself. That history still shows up today, in confidence, pay, super, investing, and the quiet feeling that everyone else somehow got the manual. So before we talk about confidence, we need to talk about context.

How we got here, in Australia
1966 Marriage Bar

Until 1966, women in the Australian Public Service were required to resign the moment they married.

1969 Equal Pay Case

Before the equal pay cases, women were legally paid 54¢ on the dollar. The gap narrowed only after legal challenge.

1984 Sex Discrimination Act

Until 1984, women could still face financial discrimination, including loan access barriers without a male guarantor.

1992 Compulsory Super

Until 1992, superannuation was built around a male, full-time career. Women were excluded by design.

Sources: Parliamentary Education Office, marriage bar abolished; Fair Work Commission, equal pay history; National Museum of Australia, Sex Discrimination Act 1984; APRA, Superannuation Guarantee introduced in 1992.

Policy · May 2026 · Her Field Notes #01

When the Government Plays House
With Your House Money

5 min read · herlonggame.com.au
⚠️ Field Note Disclaimer (The Fun Kind)
Not a political opinion. Not financial advice. Just an observer who's figured out that sometimes learning how the system works, and giving you a humorous interpretation of it, is the only way to stay sane. Because honestly, who has time to be brilliant in their own field and moonlight as an economist just to keep up? May as well laugh along the way.

Every May, the federal government presents us with their version of a household budget meeting, except instead of arguing over whether we can afford the good cheese this week, they're deciding how to spend roughly half a trillion dollars of money that technically belongs to all of us, while somehow making it feel like we're still not invited to the actual conversation.

Field Observation #1

The federal budget is essentially the government's annual attempt to explain why your taxes went up but your services somehow got more expensive. It's like having a housemate who takes your grocery money, buys organic everything for themselves, then explains that the communal toilet paper budget had to be "optimised for efficiency", which is why you're now rationing squares.

Field Observation #2

Budget night coverage treats economic policy like a sporting event, complete with winners, losers, and colour commentary from people who definitely don't do their own grocery shopping. Meanwhile, you're sitting there trying to figure out if "bracket creep relief" means you can finally afford the name-brand pasta sauce, or if it's just another way of saying "congratulations, your pay rise got eaten by a tax system."

Field Observation #3

The most fascinating part isn't what gets announced, it's what gets buried in the fine print. Big fanfare about childcare subsidies, quiet shuffling about when they actually start. Loud promises about housing affordability, whispered details about eligibility criteria that somehow exclude half the people who actually need affordable housing. It's like being promised a chocolate cake and receiving a recipe.

Field Observation #4

Every budget comes with economic forecasts that read like horoscopes written by people who've never had to choose between heating and eating. "Growth is expected to moderate while inflation remains within target" translates roughly to "things might get more expensive but we're optimistic you'll figure it out." The confidence is admirable. The disconnect is breathtaking.

Field Observation #5

The budget's treatment of women's economic security is like watching someone try to fix a leaky roof by repainting the ceiling. Superannuation changes that might help in thirty years, parental leave adjustments that assume you can afford to take leave in the first place. Systematic change at the speed of bureaucracy, which is to say, glacial.

Her Notes
  • The federal budget is a massive resource allocation exercise disguised as political theatre
  • Most budget measures have implementation delays that stretch well beyond the announcement fanfare
  • Economic forecasts are educated guesses, not guarantees, plan accordingly
  • Real impact on household budgets often takes months or years to materialise
  • The gap between policy intention and lived experience remains frustratingly wide
The Economist's View

The federal budget is a sophisticated macroeconomic tool designed to balance fiscal responsibility with economic stimulus, carefully calibrated to support growth while maintaining sustainable debt levels.

The Woman Living Inside It

The federal budget is an annual reminder that the people making decisions about your money live in a completely different economic reality, where $3 coffees are a policy talking point, not a budget line item.

Her Move

Read the budget papers themselves, not just the headlines. The Treasury website publishes the actual documents, they're surprisingly readable once you skip the political preamble. Focus on implementation dates and eligibility criteria for anything that might affect your household. Knowledge is power, and in this case, it's also the difference between planning for change and being surprised by it.

Jargon Decoder · Vol. 1 · Her Field Notes #02

The Financial Terms They Made Up
to Sound Important

3 min read · herlonggame.com.au
Welcome to the Jargon Decoder.
Finance has its own language, and it was designed to keep you out of the conversation. This is a plain-English translation service. Not financial advice. Just a glossary with a raised eyebrow.

Here's the thing about financial jargon: most of it is describing straightforward ideas in unnecessarily complicated ways. Some of it is protective camouflage for concepts the industry doesn't want you to feel confident about. Let's fix that.

The Official Terms vs. What They Actually Mean

Quantitative Easing→The government printing money and hoping no one notices.
Negative Real Returns→Your savings account is quietly going backwards after inflation.
Fiat Currency→Money that's worth something because the government says so. (Not backed by gold. Not backed by anything. Just vibes.)
Asset Appreciation→Your thing went up in price. Might mean you're richer. Might just be inflation. See: the index below.
Portfolio Diversification→Don't put all your eggs in one basket. (They made it sound fancy. It's the basket metaphor.)
Real Wages→Your pay vs. prices, the only metric that actually matters.
Fiscal Policy→Government spending, but make it bureaucratic.
Monetary Policy→The RBA making life more expensive so you spend less.
Soft Landing→Economists hoping the plane doesn't crash.
Hard Landing→Economists politely warning the plane might crash.
Sound Money→Currency that can't be endlessly inflated by governments. Scarce. Honest. Bitcoin and gold are examples. (We'll go deep on this in the app.)
Purchasing Power→What your money can actually buy. The thing inflation silently destroys. Measure it in Bubble O' Bills.
Her Move

Next time someone uses financial jargon in a meeting, a presentation, or a headline, ask yourself: what is the plain-English version of this? Usually the reason it's wrapped in complexity is because the plain-English version is either embarrassingly simple or deliberately inconvenient to say out loud.
